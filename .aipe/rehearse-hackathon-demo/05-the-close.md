# Chapter 5 — The close (8:45–9:30)

Forty-five seconds to end on a beat instead of trailing off into "yeah, so, that's it." The close has three jobs: point at where this goes next (clearly framed as future, never demoed as if it exists), make a specific ask, and land one last sentence the room repeats to each other afterward. Then stop talking — finishing at 9:30 with the room nodding beats filling the clock to 10:00.

```
  │ ░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░▓▓▓░░░░░░░░░░░ │

  │     THE CLOSE — you own 8:45 to 9:30 (45 sec)         │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Three beats — vision, ask, last line — then silence and a buffer to spare.

```
  THE CLOSE — three beats, then stop

  VISION   "today it's a neighborhood; the engine scales to a
            city with a spatial index and contraction hierarchies"
            (FUTURE — say it, never demo it as if it's built)
        │
        ▼
  ASK      what you want from THIS room: feedback on the routes
            from anyone who scoots/walks/rolls, or a vote
        │
        ▼
  LAST     the one sentence they repeat to each other
  LINE     ┃ "Shortest isn't always best — flattr routes for
              the hill you don't want to climb."
```

The vision shows you know where it goes; the ask gives the room a job; the last line is the souvenir they carry out.

## The vision (framed as future)

```
  SAY (out loud)                        SHOW (on screen)
  ──────────────────────────────        ──────────────────────────────
  "Right now it's one neighborhood.      back to the working route on
   The engine itself scales — a           screen (the REAL thing), not
   spatial index for snapping and          a mockup of the future
   contraction hierarchies take it
   city-wide."
```

Say "the engine scales" while showing the *working* neighborhood — never flash a fake city-wide screen. The honesty is the credibility; demoing vaporware as if it exists is the one move that sinks you in Q&A.

## The ask

Be specific about what you want from the room — a vague "thanks for watching" wastes the moment:

┃ "If you walk, scoot, or roll — try a route in a neighborhood you know and tell me if it picked the way you'd actually go. That's the feedback I want."

That ask doubles as your real success metric (route plausibility from a real user) — you're recruiting the discovery you actually need.

| WEAK CLOSE | STRONG CLOSE |
|---|---|
| "So yeah, that's flattr, there's a lot more I could add, thanks for watching." | "The engine scales city-wide from here. If you scoot or roll, try a route you know and tell me if it's the way you'd go. Shortest isn't always best — flattr routes for the hill you don't want to climb." |
| **Why it's weak:** trails off, hedges ("a lot more I could add"), no ask, no memorable line. The room forgets it by the next demo. | **Why it works:** future framed honestly, a concrete ask, and a last line that compresses the whole product. Ends on a beat. |

## The last line

┃ "Shortest isn't always best — flattr routes for the hill you don't want to climb."

Say it, then stop. Let it hang. The instinct to keep talking past your closer is what turns a clean ending into a mumble — resist it.

## Tighten it

Cut the vision beat first — go straight from the demo's momentum to the ask and the last line. **Floor:** the last line. Never cut your closer; it's the sentence the judges carry into deliberation.

## One-page run sheet

- **Budget:** 8:45–9:30 (45 sec). Finish by 9:30, leave the buffer.
- **SAY, in order:** vision ("engine scales city-wide — spatial index + contraction hierarchies", shown over the *real* route) → ask ("if you scoot/roll, try a route you know, tell me if it's the way you'd go") → last line.
- **Nail this line:** ┃ "Shortest isn't always best — flattr routes for the hill you don't want to climb."
- **SHOW:** the working neighborhood route. Never a mocked-up future screen.
- **If it breaks:** spoken close, no screen needed; deliver the last line to the room.
- **Tighten:** drop the vision, keep ask + last line. Floor: the last line, always.
